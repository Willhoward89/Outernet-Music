[Verse 1 — A: The Present Structure]

MENTIONED AS: "Cooper Union" — "Seven East Seventh Street, the Foundation Building stands" → Directly from: "COOPER UNION FOR THE ADVANCEMENT OF SCIENCE AND ART Third Avenue at East 7th Street… Cooper Union is located in the heart of the East Village. It was founded in 1859 by Peter Cooper." Israelowitz. (2014) Jewish New York City, p. 44.

MENTIONED AS: "The Cooper Union for the Advancement of Science and Art" — "Italianate brownstone, heavy-handed masonry bands" → Directly from: "The facade is in the Italianate brownstone tradition popular at the time with cast-iron designers, but heavier-handed, as it is in masonry except at the ground floor." AIA. (1988) AIA Edition 3, p. 155.

MENTIONED AS: "Cooper Institute" — "Seven-story brick with elevators, the land books all confirm" → Directly from: "In the 1955 Manhattan Land Book, on 7 East 7th Street, there is written 'Cooper Institute' as well as reference to 1 Structure; 7 story brick building with elevators." Bromley. 1955 Manhattan Land Book, p. 23.

MENTIONED AS: "Cooper Union" — "Segmental arched openings where rectangles held firm" → Directly from: "the replacement in 1886 of the original rectangular second-floor windows with segmental arched openings (Leopold Eidlitz, architect)." Postal & Dolkart. (2009) Guide to New York City Landmarks, p. 65.

MENTIONED AS: "Cooper Union" — "Cast-iron storefronts restored, new pieces cast aluminum" → Directly from: "In 1975-76 the cast-iron storefronts were restored (new pieces were cast in aluminum by the school's art students)." Postal & Dolkart. (2009) Guide to New York City Landmarks, p. 65.

MENTIONED AS: "Cooper Union Foundation Building" — "Corbusian white interior, a box placed deep within" → Directly from: "if the whole thing resembles, as Russell Sturgis wrote long ago of McKim, Mead & White's Century Club, 'a box with a pretty inside put into a larger box with a pretty outside,' that makes it no less valid… the Corbusian vocabulary need not be an entity unto itself." Goldberger. (1979) The City Observed, p. 69.

MENTIONED AS: "Cooper Union" — "Stainless steel tube rides the cylindrical shaft at last" → Directly from: "Almost symbolically, Peter Cooper's round elevator shaft is finally fitted with a stainless steel tube. 'My joy is boundless,' says Mr. Hejduk." Huxtable. (1976) Kicked a Building Lately?, p. 236.

MENTIONED AS: "Cooper Union" — "A round car finally functions — but the seam points to the past" → Drawn from: "A good old building should develop layers of esthetic meaning like the rings of a tree, continually enriched, rather than violated, by contemporary functions… What no longer exists is still suggested in spirit. Calculated new-old contrasts add an extra dimension to the building." Huxtable. (1976) Kicked a Building Lately?, p. 233.

[Verse 2 — B: The Deepest Rupture, 1859]

MENTIONED AS: "The Cooper Union for the Advancement of Science and Art" — "Eighteen fifty-nine, Peterson lays the iron frame" → Directly from: "A high-rise brownstone, Cooper Union is the oldest extant building framed with steel beams in America… 1859. Frederick A. Peterson." AIA. (1988) AIA Edition 3, p. 155.

MENTIONED AS: "Cooper Union Foundation Building" — "Wrought-iron railroad rails as beams — no building built the same" → Directly from: "This is the oldest iron-framed building still standing in the United States, but these aren't ordinary construction beams… He rolled some for this building, too, stretching them across brick arches to support the massive brownstone building." Harris. (2002) One Thousand New York Buildings, p. 96.

MENTIONED AS: "The Cooper Union for the Advancement of Science and Art" — "Brick arches spring from rail to rail, bearing walls of stone" → Directly from: "Such rails were used by Cooper as beams, spanning brick bearing walls. In turn, brick floor arches jumped between rail and rail." AIA. (1988) AIA Edition 3, p. 155.

MENTIONED AS: "Cooper Union" — "A round elevator shaft for a device as yet unknown" → Drawn from: "Cooper's construction allowed for a round elevator shaft for the future elevator in his belief that elevators, like pistons in a cylinder, would achieve a cylindrical shape." White. (1987) New York A Physical History, p. 167.

MENTIONED AS: "Cooper Union Foundation Building" — "Cooper rolled the rails in Trenton, stretched them standard length" → Directly from: "He rolled some for this building, too, stretching them across brick arches to support the massive brownstone building, the dimensions of which were dictated by the standard length of his rails." Harris. (2002) One Thousand New York Buildings, p. 96.

MENTIONED AS: "Cooper Union Foundation Building" — "The dimensions of the building dictated by their strength" → Directly from: "the dimensions of which were dictated by the standard length of his rails." Harris. (2002) One Thousand New York Buildings, p. 96.

MENTIONED AS: "Cooper Union" — "Nine hundred seats below the street to obviate a panic's toll" → Directly from: "The auditorium, seating 1,900—one of the two most famous and historic public halls still standing in America… he placed slightly below the level of the street, to obviate loss of life by panic." Harlow. (1931) The Old Bowery Days, p. 335.

MENTIONED AS: "Peter Cooper Institute" — "Skylights lighting more than just the top floor — lighting the whole soul" → Drawn from: "In the 1853 Perris Maps, on 7 East 7th Street, there is written 'Peter Cooper Institute' as well as reference to 1 Structure; Brick or Stone Structure with skylights lighting more than the top floor." Perris. 1853 Perris Maps, p. 49.

[Verse 3 — A': Return, Now Weighted]

MENTIONED AS: "Cooper Union" — "Seven East Seventh — brownstone shell, Renaissance disguise" → Drawn from: "Cooper Union is now the best of both worlds. Its 'Renaissance' shell is intact." Huxtable. (1976) Kicked a Building Lately?, p. 236.

MENTIONED AS: "Cooper Union" — "Hejduk gutted every floor and flooded dark with light from skies" → Drawn from: "The renovation by John Hejduk, which involved a virtual gutting of the original structure, is splendid. It is white and pure as the driven snow… What was dark is now flooded with light." Huxtable. (1976) Kicked a Building Lately?, p. 233.

MENTIONED AS: "Cooper Union" — "Foliated iron columns buried deep in plaster sleeves" → Drawn from: "Compliance with current codes has hidden the fine, foliated cast-iron columns in sleek, fireproof plaster." Huxtable. (1976) Kicked a Building Lately?, p. 233.

MENTIONED AS: "Cooper Union" — "What was closed is now expanded — spatial geometry believes" → Directly from: "What was closed and fragmented is now an expanded spatial geometry." Huxtable. (1976) Kicked a Building Lately?, p. 233.

MENTIONED AS: "Cooper Union" — "The round shaft stands as sculpture played against Victorian glass" → Directly from: "the round elevator shaft stands virtually as abstract sculpture, played against the light-filled drama of the Victorian windows." Huxtable. (1976) Kicked a Building Lately?, p. 233.

MENTIONED AS: "Cooper Union Foundation Building" — "A pretty inside box placed in a larger box — both built to last" → Directly from: "if the whole thing resembles, as Russell Sturgis wrote long ago of McKim, Mead & White's Century Club, 'a box with a pretty inside put into a larger box with a pretty outside,' that makes it no less valid." Goldberger. (1979) The City Observed, p. 69.

MENTIONED AS: "The Cooper Union for the Advancement of Science and Art" — "The oldest iron-framed building in America still breathes" → Directly from: "Cooper Union is the oldest extant building framed with steel beams in America." AIA. (1988) AIA Edition 3, p. 155.

MENTIONED AS: "Cooper Union" — "But the rails inside the walls remain — mute iron underneath" → Drawn from: "Cooper's construction allowed for a round elevator shaft for the future elevator… Meanwhile he'd been active in city politics… the wrought-iron beams were designed and made for Cooper Union." White. (1987) New York A Physical History, p. 167.
